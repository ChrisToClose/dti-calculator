How does purchase price affect your DTI?
The higher the price, the bigger the loan, the higher your monthly payment — and the more it eats into your DTI. Every $50,000 more in purchase price adds roughly $250–350/mo to your payment depending on your rate and term. That extra payment has to come from your income allowance.

How does down payment affect your DTI?
More down = smaller loan = lower payment = lower DTI. It also affects PMI — put down less than 20% and you'll pay Private Mortgage Insurance monthly. Putting 20%+ down eliminates PMI entirely. A larger down payment is one of the most powerful levers you have to qualify for a more expensive home.

What is HOA and why does it count against you?
HOA stands for Homeowners Association — monthly fees for shared amenities. Lenders count HOA fees as part of your housing payment. A $500/mo HOA is treated exactly like $500 more in mortgage payment. High HOA fees in condos or gated communities can significantly reduce how much home you qualify for.

What is PMI?
Private Mortgage Insurance — required when you put down less than 20%. It typically adds 0.5%–1.5% of the loan per year to your monthly payment. Once you reach 20% equity you can request it removed. The best way to avoid it is to put 20% or more down.

What is flood insurance and when is it required?
If a property is in a FEMA flood zone, your lender requires flood insurance — mandatory, not optional. Standard homeowners insurance does NOT cover floods. In Florida, many properties near water or low-lying areas require it. Cost ranges from $150–$500+/mo depending on flood risk and elevation.

What is DTI and why does 50% matter?
DTI = Debt-to-Income ratio. It compares your total monthly debt payments to your gross monthly income.

Front-End = housing payment ÷ income.
Back-End = all monthly debts ÷ income.

Most loans require back-end DTI at 50% or below. Think of it as: for every dollar you earn, no more than 50 cents can go toward debt payments. Lower DTI = stronger application = better rate options.
